# Sara AI — The Problem Our Prospects Actually Have

Why UK dental clinics need an AI receptionist. This is the ground truth behind every
email and every close. Use it to speak to real pain — never to invent numbers.

---

## The core problem: clinics lose new patients at the phone, before anyone books

A new patient rarely waits. When they call and no one picks up, they don't leave a
voicemail and sit tight — they hang up and ring the next practice on Google. The
front desk is the leak, and it leaks hardest at exactly the moments a busy practice
can't help it: mid-treatment, lunchtime, and after hours.

### What the research shows (third-party — cite as "industry research", never as our own data)
- The average UK dental practice misses roughly **15–30% of inbound calls** during
  peak times, lunch, after hours, and when the desk is with a patient.
- Around **80% of missed calls are booking requests** — i.e. money walking away.
- When a new patient hits voicemail, the large majority **hang up and call a
  competitor** instead of leaving a message.
- For a practice missing ~10 new-patient calls a week, that's a meaningful five-figure
  monthly value at risk.

Sources: meetlinda.ai, resonateapp.com, dentalintel.com, o3dental.com (industry blogs —
directional, not gospel; cite carefully and never as Sara's own measured results).

## The second problem: the receptionist itself is expensive and unstable

This is why "actively hiring a receptionist" is our buying signal — the pain is live.

- Front-desk tenure in dental has fallen from ~3–4 years to ~1.5–2.5 years since 2019.
  Practices are re-hiring the same seat repeatedly.
- Top reasons staff leave: pay, burnout/workload, better offers elsewhere.
- Every departure means a recruitment cost, a training gap, and a stretch where calls
  go unanswered and the remaining team is overloaded.
- A receptionist also only covers ~40 hours. Evenings, weekends and sickness are
  uncovered — and that's when a chunk of new-patient calls actually come in.

A clinic posting a receptionist role is, right now, feeling all of this.

## How this maps to what Sara actually does (all true — no overclaiming)

| Their problem | What Sara genuinely does |
|---|---|
| Calls missed when the desk is with a patient | Answers every call, in parallel, no hold |
| After-hours / weekend callers lost | Answers 24/7 |
| New patients hang up on voicemail | A real conversation, books them in there and then |
| Receptionist turnover + re-hiring cost | No salary, no notice period, no rehiring cycle |
| Front desk overloaded | Takes the routine calls so the team handles the room |

What Sara is **not**: a replacement for a warm human on a busy morning. Always frame
her as catching the calls the team physically can't get to — that's honest and it
disarms the "I don't want a robot" objection.

## How this sharpens the outreach (the messaging rules)

1. **Lead with their moment, not our product.** "Noticed you're hiring a receptionist"
   works because the pain is already on their mind. Only use it when it's true.
2. **Name the specific leak, not a stat.** "the call that comes in while everyone's
   with a patient" beats "you miss 27% of calls" — it's vivid, true, and unarguable.
   Avoid quoting precise percentages in emails; they invite "says who?" and some of
   the figures are US/blog-sourced.
3. **Frame as additive, not replacement.** Protects against the robot objection.
4. **De-risk the decision.** First month free, cancel anytime — let them prove it on
   their own real calls.
5. **One next step.** Hear the demo, or a 15-minute setup call. Never two asks.

---

## Honesty guardrails (halal)

- Never present industry stats as Sara's own results or as a specific clinic's numbers.
- Never invent a clinic-specific figure ("you're losing £X").
- The logic — busy/after-hours calls get missed, missed new patients go elsewhere — is
  self-evidently true and needs no statistic. Lead with the logic.
- Every claim about what Sara does must be something she actually does today.
